A living portrait of Cortes Island

This is an experiment in making a place legible: one island, its news, wildlife, weather, tides, history and lore, gathered into a single view you can fly around. It is also, deliberately, an experiment in asking whether that is a good idea.

Where the data comes from

Live feeds: local and regional news (Cortes Currents, Campbell River Mirror, Google News), iNaturalist wildlife observations, Wikipedia and Wikimedia Commons, OpenStreetMap places, Openverse and Flickr photos, Mastodon, Open-Meteo weather, and Canadian Hydrographic Service tide predictions from the Whaletown station. CKTZ 89.5, the island's community radio, streams live through the radio button.

Curated layers: territory acknowledgment and cultural anchors; an "island almanac" of dated places and events; deep cuts of history, chart lore and marine hazards; and habitat spots. Much of this was mined from a frontier language model's world knowledge, then hand-reviewed — every such item is labeled, carries a confidence flag (documented / reported / oral tradition / legend), and lives in versioned files anyone can correct.

Should an island be transparent?

Total legibility is an old dream — and an old danger. Mapping has never been neutral: the 1860 Admiralty survey that marked south Cortes as "open prairie land" was erasing Indigenous cultivation even as it recorded the shoreline, and the settler names it fixed overwrote ʔayʔaǰuθəm ones within a generation. States and companies have long used legibility to tax, extract and dispossess; James C. Scott called this "seeing like a state." A perfect real-time portrait of a small community could be a gift — or a surveillance instrument, a gentrification brochure, a harvest map for poachers pointed straight at eagle nests and clam beds.

There is also a counter-tradition this project tries to stand in: social cartography and counter-mapping — communities mapping themselves, on their own terms, to hold ground rather than lose it. Indigenous nations have led here, from counter-maps asserting title to the First Nations principles of OCAP® (ownership, control, access, possession) and the CARE principles for Indigenous data governance: collective benefit, authority to control, responsibility, ethics. The lesson generalizes: data about a place belongs, first, to the people who live it.

Our working answers, imperfect and revisable: use only public or freely licensed sources; link everything to its origin; label speculation and lore as such; treat culturally sensitive sites with care (some things are named but deliberately not precisely located); keep the whole pipeline open source so the portrait can be audited, corrected, or forked by the people portrayed. What this project does not yet have — and what would matter most — is genuine participation: islanders, and the Klahoose, Tla'amin and Homalco nations whose territory this is, deciding what belongs here, contributing their own layers, and owning the result. Transparency without consent is extraction; transparency with stewardship might be memory.

If you live on Cortes and want something added, corrected, or removed — the door is open.
