# A living portrait of Cortes Island

*July 2026 · [cortes.exe.xyz](https://cortes.exe.xyz) · [source](https://github.com/orbitalfoundation/cortes-island)*

Forced away from a birthday hike due to inclement weather, and with a friend whose seaplane was also cancelled due to the same inclementness, we ended up spending a few very enjoyable days on Cortes Island in the care of friends adjacent to Hollyhock. Cortes turns out to be an island in the Discovery Islands archipelago on the coast of British Columbia, Canada. A small island, I keep on bumping into far ends of it. The car geography feels miniaturized; I accidentally over-drive or over-jog, and keep running into the same people over and over. One of the founders of Greenpeace runs a festival [Cortez Love Fest](https://ourcortes.com/blog/2025/07/15/cortes-love-fest-august-9-2025/) here in the summer, the other 1034 residents seem to largely be divided between larger coastal homes and a conglomerate of mixed structures distributed through the rough interior. Hollyhock itself seems to be a Friedrich Wilhelm Joseph Schelling point; with financial derivative academics, Iranians discussing the war, and at least one groovy of hippies. Everything is bursting with green, and size, supersized flowers, fruits, berries, almost like traveling to a prehistoric world. Coasts are beautiful; tide-pools, starfish, crabs, otters with their little pawprints, heron - and in the inlets the spouts of whale and young orca fins. The 'office of the Klahoose First Nation' is here but invisible to me - and the names are spanish - in this case a Hernán Cortés who never made it up here. Anecdotally Marina Island, just southwest of Cortes Island in Sutil Channel, was named for La Malinche or Doña Marina a Nahua woman who was 'interpreter, adviser, and mistress' of Hernando Cortes.

Claude and I spent a day then building a 3D portrait of Cortes Island that aggregates everything we could find: people, places, things, events, myths, shipwrecks, tide-data, ferry schedules, whale sightings and we draped it over photorealistic tiles, adding weather, sounds and even cartoonified versions of the ferries, seaplanes and other entities.

## What it is

The goals is a photorealistic model of the island with its data cloud. Google's 3D tiles, pulled through Cesium ion (but rendered entirely in three.js) — under a sky computed from the actual sun and moon positions for the island's coordinates. Real weather (Open-Meteo), so overcast in Whaletown means overcast on screen, and the sea state follows the wind. Tide line data in the corner comes from the federal hydrographic station at Whaletown. At night the settlements glow. The BC Ferries run to Heriot Bay crosses on roughly its actual schedule, a CorilAir floatplane arrives from Vancouver around five, gulls circle Sutil Point, and a humpback surfaces in Sutil Channel if you wait.

Over this we draped data: floating cards, color-coded by category, tethered to their coordinates. News from the local outlets. Wildlife observations from iNaturalist, with photographs. Freely licensed photos from Wikimedia Commons and Openverse. Community posts from Mastodon and Reddit. Landmarks from Wikipedia and OpenStreetMap. Near the ground, a second tier of small markers: stores, trailheads, marine hazards, where the herons are.

## Fun technical features

A fastify server aggregates on a fifteen-minute tick. Each source adapter emits raw items onto a pub/sub bus (`@orbitalfoundation/bus` — the same single-channel pattern I use everywhere); an enrichment stage geolocates and categorizes them; a store listener persists to Mongo and fans out changes over socket.io to connected clients. Geolocation is tiered: exact coordinates when the source has them, then a gazetteer of island place names, then a language model pass that reads the text, judges whether it's really about Cortes, scores importance, and extracts named places. Items that resolve only to "the island" get scattered deterministically near settlements — an early version scattered them around the island's centroid, which put them all in Hague Lake, since the centroid of an island is the one place nobody publishes from.

Deduplication turned out to be the interesting problem. The same real-world thing arrives as an OpenStreetMap node, a Wikipedia article, a news story, and a curated fact, each with a different URL. Title-token similarity plus geographic proximity catches most of it, with one essential exemption: items from the same source never merge, because two iNaturalist sightings of the same species are different events, not duplicates.

The front-end is fun as well. three.js + 3d-tiles-renderer, fastify + socket.io, Mongo, an event bus, Claude for enrichment, docker compose on a small VM. No CesiumJS, no map framework, no frontend framework. About 4,000 lines. Most of the visual richness — ocean shader, procedural clouds, sun and moon math, the soundscape of surf, wind and gull cries — is small self-contained modules with no assets; the only textures on the wire are the earth itself and the photographs in the cards. The radio button plays CKTZ 89.5, the island's community station, live.

## Working away in the knowledge mines

One of my favorite hacks ( which we also used in https://divesight.ai ) is to directly ask a frontier language model about a place. In this case I was able to find about 180 dated, geolocated facts about this island of a thousand people. The 1869 whaling station, the unsolved murder on Twin Islands, the Queen's two visits, the clam gardens, the post-glacial shorelines 197 meters up in the forest, which harbour bottoms won't hold an anchor. Spot-checking found them largely right. These went in as versioned JSON with confidence labelsb(documented / reported / oral tradition / legend) and a visible provenance tag, not laundered into looking like scraped truth. The files are in the repo as raw data.

## On making an island legible

Whether a place *should* be this visible is a real question, and the project carries an essay about it in the about panel. Mapping has never been neutral — the 1860 Admiralty survey that charted these shores marked Indigenous garden sites as empty "prairie land" — and a real-time portrait of a small community could serve extraction as easily as memory. The counter-tradition is community self-mapping, and the standard to aim at is the one Indigenous data governance articulates (OCAP, CARE): the people, the stakeholders, those affected, those portrayed should control the portrait. At some level we're all human, we all are embodied, we are all vulnerable in some ways - some less than others - how do we protect what is valuable from bad actors? Keeping knowledge hermetic is one way. I'd like to discuss this more if anybody is interested. In this case this build uses public sources, links everything to origin, labels the speculative, and is open source. What it lacks is the part that matters most: the island's own participation.

## The future

We can skry the future. In a few years this won't even be worth writing. Frontier models will simply produce experiences like this on demand - applications won't even exist anymore. We'll just be pouring our understanding into a share jungian unconsciousness. I also fully expect future tools to go much further than this - to generate predictive models; to show the intersection of land use, law and policy, to compute forward the next hundred years of this special place. I don't have any answers here, no nice conclusions to wrap with, I simply note that the future for all of us is quite different from our past.
